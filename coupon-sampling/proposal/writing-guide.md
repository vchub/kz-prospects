Here is a grant-proposal guide reverse engineered from McEnerney’s core claims about value, readers, and “knowledge” as something communities decide to count.

What I am assuming (and how to sanity-check it)

1. Assumption: the proposal’s primary audience is a specific review community, not a generic “scientist”

- McEnerney is explicit that value is assessed by quite specific readers, and that “basic structure and argument” cannot be audience-free. ([WPMU CDN][1])
- Sanity-check: read the funder’s review criteria and panel descriptions; skim abstracts/titles of recently funded projects to infer what that community rewards.

1. Assumption: rejection is often a reading-process failure, not a “bad idea”

- McEnerney’s handout explicitly says mismatch between your writing process and their reading process can get work rejected for funding, even if it felt clarifying to you. ([WPMU CDN][1])
- Sanity-check: ask two colleagues outside your sub-subfield to summarize your “why it matters” after 2 minutes. If they cannot, reviewers likely will not either.

1. Assumption: novelty alone is weak currency

- His lecture’s central example is that “new/original” can be trivial if it produces no value for the reader community. ([The Singju Post][2])
- Sanity-check: explicitly name what becomes easier, faster, safer, or newly possible if funded, and for whom.

A McEnerney-style objective function for grants
Think like you are optimizing perceived value under severe attention constraints:

[
V_{\text{review}} = B_{\text{community}} - C_{\text{cognitive}} - R_{\text{risk}}
]

Where:

- (B\_{\text{community}}) = benefit to what the review community already cares about (not what you care about)
- (C\_{\text{cognitive}}) = effort to understand what you are doing and why it matters
- (R\_{\text{risk}}) = perceived likelihood of failure, ambiguity, or overreach

This is consistent with his “stop thinking about rules, start thinking about readers” framing. ([The Singju Post][2]) and with the explicit “function and value” claim that academic writing should help readers understand something they already want to understand well, and that readers must be able to perceive that quickly. ([WPMU CDN][1])

Step-by-step: build the proposal from the reviewers’ incentives backward

Step 1: Identify the real “they”
Do not start from your topic. Start from the decision-makers.

- Who are the likely reviewers, program staff, and the funder’s implied mission audience?
- What do they routinely label as “important” or “impactful” (their vocabulary, not yours)?
- What are the common failure modes they penalize (over-ambition, unclear feasibility, unclear mechanism, weak evaluation plan)?

If you are applying to NIH-like schemes, note that criteria are explicitly framed around importance, rigor/feasibility, and expertise/resources (under the simplified framework for many due dates on/after Jan 25, 2025). ([Grants.gov][3])

Deliverable of Step 1

- A one-paragraph “reviewer profile”:
  - they care about X because Y
  - they doubt claims like Z unless they see evidence type E
  - they punish A, reward B

Step 2: Draft the “value-first” opening (your most important paragraph)
McEnerney’s practical implication is that the opening must rapidly create value for that reader community, not merely state a topic or a gap. ([WPMU CDN][1])

A workable structure:

- Shared stake: What problem do they already recognize as costly or limiting?
- Cost of the status quo: What is failing, slow, noisy, biased, non-generalizable, or too expensive right now?
- Your promise: What will become possible if your approach works (specific capability, not vague “insight”)?
- Credibility signal: One concrete reason they should believe you can deliver (prelim data, unique access, proven method, prior result).

Critical constraint

- If your first page reads like “background, then eventually the point,” you are betting on reviewer patience. That is a bad bet.

Step 3: Turn “knowledge” into “fundable knowledge”
McEnerney’s model implies knowledge = novelty filtered by a community’s valuation, with gatekeeping power deciding what counts. ([The Singju Post][2])

So, for every “new” claim, attach a value claim that is legible to the community:

- New measurement = reduces uncertainty in decision D
- New mechanism = unlocks intervention lever L
- New method = increases throughput, lowers cost, improves reproducibility
- New dataset = enables test of contested claim C

A harsh test you should apply

- If your “innovation” can be removed and the proposal still sounds like it matters, then the innovation is probably decorative.
- If your “significance” can be removed and the proposal still sounds technically impressive, you have written for yourself, not for them.

Step 4: Write the middle as risk management, not exposition
Grant reviewers are professionally trained skeptics. Your job is not to describe everything you know; it is to make it easy to say “yes” with minimal cognitive and reputational risk.

Use a “claim, evidence, consequence” loop:

- Claim: what you will do
- Evidence: why it will work (prelim results, citations, prior demonstrations)
- Consequence: what you will know at the end, and what decision that enables

Make uncertainty explicit and bounded

- State what would count as failure early, and what you will do instead (plan B that still yields value).
- Review research shows peer review can be noisy and variable, so clarity and feasibility signals matter because reviewers are not perfectly consistent. ([Springer][4])

Step 5: Design aims as “information production,” not “tasks”
Each aim should answer:

- What question will be resolved?
- What would change in the field if it is resolved?
- What would you do next depending on the result?

Bad aim pattern

- “Develop X” without specifying decision-relevant output, validation target, or success criteria.

Better aim pattern

- “Determine whether X improves Y under constraints Z, using benchmark B; success = meeting threshold T; if not, pivot to alternative A.”

Step 6: Make the reading process easy (reduce (C\_{\text{cognitive}}))
McEnerney’s handout is blunt that even excellent work gets rejected if the reader cannot perceive the value and function of the writing. ([WPMU CDN][1])

Practical moves

- Topic sentences that state the point (not scene-setting)
- Short paragraphs with a single job
- Frequent signposts that answer “why are you telling me this now?”
- Figures that carry argumentative weight (not decorative schematics)

A proposal-level “garden test”

- Each section should prune confusion like removing weeds: if a paragraph does not increase the probability of funding, it is a weed.

Step 7: Anticipate the panel conversation
Panels do social calibration, and review outcomes can depend on discussion dynamics, not only content. ([Springer][4])
You cannot control that, but you can shape what is easy to repeat aloud:

- Give reviewers short, repeatable sentences for:
  - the problem
  - the innovation
  - the feasibility proof
  - the expected payoff

- Include 1–2 “sound-bite” results you will deliver (quantified, benchmarked)

Step 8: Build a “reviewer memo” and make the proposal match it
Write a one-page internal memo that a friendly reviewer could read to argue for you:

- Overall impact in 3 sentences
- 3 strengths, 2 manageable weaknesses (with your mitigations)
- What would be lost if not funded

Then revise the proposal until that memo is an accurate summary.

Common McEnerney-aligned failure modes (and how to attack them)

1. “We don’t see why we should care”

- Fix: move stakes and payoff earlier; tie to reviewer community goals. ([WPMU CDN][1])

1. “Unclear what is actually being done”

- Fix: convert prose into explicit claims and tests; add success criteria and decision points.

1. “Too risky / too ambitious”

- Fix: tighten scope; add milestones; show contingency paths that still generate value.

1. “Technically impressive, but not valuable”

- Fix: force every method detail to earn its place by linking to a valued outcome (cost, speed, accuracy, generality, reproducibility).
