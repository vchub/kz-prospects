# Decision Memo: Top Sampling Pains for Coca-Cola in Kazakhstan / CIS

## Purpose

This memo compresses the broader research into a decision-oriented view:

- what the biggest sampling pains are for Coca-Cola and promo agencies
- how agencies and brands currently try to solve them
- where the proposed Telegram / digital coupon model helps most
- where it still does not fully solve the problem

---

## Top 5 pains for Coca-Cola

## 1. Physical logistics and cold-chain complexity

### What the problem actually is

In beverage sampling, the issue is not just moving product from warehouse to point A.
It includes:

- delivering product to promo locations
- keeping it cold
- storing it securely
- managing stock at each location
- replacing missing or depleted stock
- ensuring product is available exactly where media sends users

For Kazakhstan / CIS this is worse because logistics costs are volatile, warehouse capacity is constrained in some corridors, and border delays create operational unpredictability.

### Why this matters to Coca-Cola

- sampling costs rise quickly
- execution gets fragile
- scale is limited by operations, not demand
- regional rollout becomes risky

### What agencies do now

- ship stock to promo points
- use coolers / fridges / temporary storage
- assign staff to track stock manually
- narrow activations to a limited number of cities or high-traffic sites

### Does that work?

Only partly.

It works for highly controlled events, but it does not scale cleanly and is expensive to supervise.

### Where the Telegram / coupon model helps

- shifts fulfillment to existing retail stock or delivery stock
- removes most cold-chain burden from the campaign itself
- reduces the need to physically stage product at every activation point

### What it still does not solve

- partner stores must still have stock
- retail execution remains outside Coca-Cola’s direct control

---

## 2. Poor measurement and weak attribution

### What the problem actually is

Classic sampling often produces activity but not reliable data.

Typical unanswered questions:

- how many real users got the sample?
- where did they come from?
- which placements worked?
- did trial convert into retail behavior?
- did some channels produce waste rather than value?

### Why this matters to Coca-Cola

- difficult to justify spend internally
- difficult to optimize campaign live
- difficult to compare sampling against other channels

### What agencies do now

- manual counts from promoters
- post-campaign reporting
- rough attendance estimates
- occasional QR landing pages

### Does that work?

Poorly compared to digital-first systems.

Manual reporting is easy to inflate, hard to audit, and rarely granular enough to optimize a live campaign.

### Where the Telegram / coupon model helps

- every voucher is tied to a user session
- activation becomes a measurable real-time event
- channel, time, and product preference can be logged
- later partner confirmation can be reconciled against activation data

### What it still does not solve

- it does not automatically prove repeat purchase or long-term lift
- it still depends on partner reconciliation for final confirmation in retail

---

## 3. Theft, shrinkage, and fraud

### What the problem actually is

This includes both physical and digital abuse:

- product disappearing at promo points
- promoters giving away stock off-record
- repeated claims by the same user
- screenshot sharing of static codes
- low-quality “freebie hunter” claims

### Why this matters to Coca-Cola

- wasted samples
- distorted campaign reporting
- poor trust in agency execution
- higher cost per real trial

### What agencies do now

- tighter supervision
- manual stock logs
- receipt checks
- one-time paper vouchers
- staff sign-off

### Does that work?

Only partially.

It lowers obvious abuse, but manual controls remain weak once product is physically present and staff are under pressure.

### Where the Telegram / coupon model helps

- one voucher per Telegram account
- code is generated only at activation
- code can expire quickly
- code is one-time use
- promo-point confirmation can be forced through a controlled interface

### What it still does not solve

- determined multi-account abuse remains possible
- staff can still bypass procedure if operational discipline is weak

---

## 4. Retail execution and availability risk

### What the problem actually is

The campaign can perform well digitally and still fail operationally if:

- the product is not on shelf
- the wrong SKU is in store
- the cashier does not understand the promo
- the store cannot process the redemption

### Why this matters to Coca-Cola

- consumer frustration
- brand damage
- wasted media spend
- tension with retail partners

### What agencies and brands do now

- restrict campaigns to selected chains
- run store checks
- use promoters to explain mechanics
- keep campaigns narrow by geography and SKU

### Does that work?

It works if the campaign is tightly controlled.
It fails when geography, SKU count, or store count become too broad for the operational discipline available.

### Where the Telegram / coupon model helps

- campaigns can be limited to specific chains
- activation can be tied to specific partner flows
- demand can be routed to channels with better operational readiness

### What it still does not solve

- it does not fix out-of-stock
- it does not train cashiers
- it does not guarantee store-level execution quality

---

## 5. Staffing burden and agency control

### What the problem actually is

Classic sampling depends on field staff:

- promoters
- supervisors
- logistics staff
- merchandisers
- people who count, report, and reconcile results

This creates staffing pressure and quality variance.

### Why this matters to Coca-Cola

- agency dependence increases
- execution quality varies by city and team
- scaling requires more staff, not just more budget

### What agencies do now

- recruit large temporary promoter pools
- use supervisors and reporting sheets
- simplify scripts and mechanics
- reduce duration or geography to fit available staffing

### Does that work?

It works operationally at small scale, but quality and transparency degrade as complexity rises.

### Where the Telegram / coupon model helps

- reduces promoter dependence in retail and delivery flows
- centralizes logic in software instead of in manual field behavior
- leaves human staff mainly for promo points, where they add real value

### What it still does not solve

- promo-point staffing still matters
- partner-side store staff are not under agency control

---

## What agencies are currently doing

Across the market, brands and agencies are moving toward four practical patterns:

### 1. Use retail or delivery as the fulfillment layer

Instead of shipping product to every promo point, they try to make existing stock do the work.

### 2. Add a digital claim layer

The user first claims or activates digitally, which improves:

- measurement
- auditability
- fraud control

### 3. Narrow the operational scope

They limit:

- cities
- chains
- SKUs
- campaign duration

This is a practical way to reduce operational failure.

### 4. Add filtering and control

They use:

- qualification logic
- unique codes
- short expiry
- receipt validation
- cashback fallback

---

## Where the proposed model helps most

The proposed Telegram / digital coupon model helps most on the top three pains:

### 1. Logistics

By moving fulfillment into retail / delivery stock, it reduces the heaviest physical burden.

### 2. Measurement

By separating `ваучер -> активация -> погашение`, it creates live operational visibility even if retail confirmation arrives later.

### 3. Fraud and theft control

By forcing activation and one-time redemption, it is much more controllable than open physical giveaway.

The model is especially strong where classic sampling is weakest:

- at promo points with poor manual control
- in campaigns where the brand wants live analytics
- in cities where operating many staffed cold-product points is expensive

---

## Where it still does not solve the problem

The model improves the system, but it does not eliminate all constraints.

### 1. Retail partner execution remains a dependency

If stores are poorly prepared, understocked, or slow to reconcile, the user still experiences friction.

### 2. Telegram is a channel constraint

The model excludes part of the audience unless a fallback entry point is added later.

### 3. Delivery partner mechanics still require discipline

Promo-code pools, order logic, and reimbursement mechanics can fail operationally if not agreed upfront.

### 4. Promo-point discipline still matters

Even with a web panel, bad on-site execution can still create leakage if staff ignore the process.

### 5. Long-term sales impact still needs separate measurement

The model improves attribution of trial and activation, but not full incremental sales proof by itself.

---

## Practical conclusion

For Coca-Cola in Kazakhstan / CIS, the main value of this model is not “digital for the sake of digital.”

The value is operational:

- less physical burden
- more control
- better auditability
- better live decision-making

This makes the model a strong answer to the biggest current weaknesses of sampling.

But it should be positioned honestly:

- it solves the hardest campaign-level problems better than classic sampling
- it does not remove the need for strong retail partner execution
- it is best launched as a tightly scoped pilot, not as a broad national mechanic from day one
