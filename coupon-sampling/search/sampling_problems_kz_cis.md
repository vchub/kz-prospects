# Sampling Problems for Coca-Cola / Promo Agencies in Kazakhstan and CIS

## Scope

This note summarizes the main operational problems brands like Coca-Cola and promo agencies face when running sampling campaigns in Kazakhstan and the CIS, with emphasis on beverage sampling and retail-linked activation.

It is based on web research across:

- Kazakhstan / CIS logistics and retail sources
- retail execution sources
- digital sampling case studies
- promotion fraud / offer infrastructure case studies

Where the evidence is direct, it is stated as such.
Where the conclusion is a synthesis or inference for beverage sampling, it is marked as such.

---

## Executive summary

The biggest problems are not creative. They are operational.

Ranked by practical importance for Kazakhstan / CIS sampling campaigns:

1. **Logistics and cold-chain execution**
2. **Measurement and attribution**
3. **Fraud, theft, and abuse**
4. **Retail execution and stock availability**
5. **Staffing and field-force reliability**
6. **Market fragmentation and channel coverage**
7. **Rising costs and supply volatility**

The strongest pattern in the market is clear:

- offline sampling is hard because physical distribution is expensive and hard to control
- digital sampling improves measurement and fraud control
- retail-linked redemption improves economics if retail stock already exists
- the weakest point remains partner operations: stock, code pools, store execution, and reconciliation

---

## Ranked problems

## 1. Logistics and cold-chain execution

### Why this ranks first

For beverage sampling, especially chilled product, logistics is the hardest operational constraint.

It is not just "delivery" in the abstract. It includes:

- moving product to the sampling point
- keeping it cold
- storing it safely
- avoiding spoilage and shrinkage
- making sure the product is actually available at the location where the campaign sends users

### Why this is especially serious in Kazakhstan / CIS

- Kazakhstan has had **warehouse shortages** due to regional logistics pressure and parallel-import rerouting via CIS markets.
- Kazakhstan also faces **border and customs delays**, which increase uncertainty and costs.
- In some food categories, analysts note that distribution systems are still weak and chain retail penetration is limited.

### Source-backed signals

- Kursiv reported a **shortage of warehouses in Kazakhstan and other CIS countries**, tied to growing logistics demand and limited available space:
  [Kazakhstan reports shortage of warehouses due to parallel import to Russia](https://kz.kursiv.media/en/2023-04-10/kazakhstan-reports-shortage-of-warehouses-due-to-parallel-import-to-russia/)
- Kursiv also cited INFOLine saying **70% of manufacturers and suppliers** see logistics obstacles as a key driver of uncertainty and rising costs:
  [More than half of food imports to Kazakhstan come from Russia](https://kz.kursiv.media/en/2023-04-14/more-than-half-of-food-imports-to-kazakhstan-come-from-russia/)
- Kazakhstan PMI reporting has linked **delivery-time delays and higher input costs** to logistics and customs problems:
  [Manufacturing industry reports increasing business activity for 12 consecutive months in Kazakhstan](https://kz.kursiv.media/en/2025-03-03/engk-tank-manufacturing-industry-reports-increasing-business-activity-for-12-consecutive-months-in-kazakhstan/)
  [Kazakhstan services activity declines amid Russian border delays](https://kz.kursiv.media/en/2025-12-03/engk-tank-kazakhstans-service-sector-reports-activity-decline-amid-delays-at-the-russian-border/)
- In Kazakhstan’s ice-cream market, analysts explicitly mention **lack of developed frozen-food distribution** and weak chain retail development as constraints:
  [Ice Cream Market in Kazakhstan – First Half of 2024](https://dairynews.today/news/ice-cream-market-in-kazakhstan-first-half-of-2024.html)

### How brands try to solve it

- Use **existing retail cold chain** instead of shipping product to temporary promo points
- Use **delivery partners** for last-mile redemption
- Run **digital voucher -> retail redemption** models so the store stock becomes the fulfillment layer

### Does it work?

Yes, partly.

The strongest evidence is from digital sampling platforms that route consumers to existing stores instead of shipping product into field activations:

- Sampl’s NearStore model is built around linking trial to nearby retail and purchase:
  [How NearStore links digital sampling with in-store sales](https://www.sampltech.com/blog/how-nearstore-links-digital-sampling-with-in-store-sales)
- Manilife used store-nearby targeting and reported **40% uplift in rate of sale** in targeted stores:
  [Manilife campaign](https://www.sampltech.com/case-studies/how-we-helped-manilife-with-their-healthy-eating-campaign)
- Bio&Me used a retail-linked coupon path and reported **18% coupon redemption**:
  [Bio&Me case](https://www.sampltech.com/case-studies/biome-18-coupon-redemption)

### Bottom line

This is the biggest operational reason to prefer **retail-linked digital sampling** over classic staffed chilled sampling in Kazakhstan.

## 2. Measurement and attribution

### Why this ranks second

Traditional sampling often ends when the product is handed out.

Brands struggle to answer:

- who received the sample?
- did the person actually try it?
- did trial lead to purchase?
- which channel / store / time window worked?

For a large FMCG brand, this is a major budget problem because sampling becomes difficult to defend internally.

### Source-backed signals

- Sampl explicitly frames the market problem as:
  - no data capture
  - no follow-up
  - no measurable path from sample to purchase
  [Sampl platform](https://www.sampltech.com/)
  [Sampl Wins Best Lead Gen Software 2025](https://www.sampltech.com/blog/sampl-named-best-lead-gen-software-uk-business-tech-awards-2025)
- SamplPay is positioned specifically as a way to prove retail sales impact **without retailer approval**:
  [SamplPay](https://www.sampltech.com/products/samplpay)
- NIQ positions on-shelf availability and in-store execution tools around the problem of **missed promotional opportunities** and lack of granular visibility:
  [OSA Barometer](https://nielseniq.com/global/en/products/osa-barometer/)
  [NIQ In-Store Vision](https://nielseniq.com/global/en/products/nielseniq-instore-vision/)

### How brands try to solve it

- digital claims before redemption
- unique coupon / code issuance
- review capture after trial
- CRM capture and opt-ins
- store-level or region-level redemption data
- cashback / receipt validation if direct coupon redemption is difficult

### Does it work?

Usually yes, when the system is digital-first.

Examples:

- CocoaVia used sampling to generate **11,909 leads** and 1,106 reviews:
  [CocoaVia case](https://www.sampltech.com/case-studies/cocoavia-crm-sampling-campaign)
- Andrex used sampling to improve review generation and filtered demand aggressively:
  [Andrex case](https://www.sampltech.com/case-studies/heres-how-we-helped-andrex-r-capture-genuine-reviews)
- SamplPay’s positioning suggests that receipt / cashback methods are being used precisely because brands want attribution without waiting for retailer integrations:
  [SamplPay](https://www.sampltech.com/products/samplpay)

### Bottom line

For both Coca-Cola and its promo agency, lack of measurement is not a side issue. It is one of the main reasons traditional sampling looks inefficient.

## 3. Fraud, theft, and abuse

### Why this ranks third

This includes several different failure modes:

- promo staff over-reporting or under-reporting
- untracked giveaway of product at promo points
- repeated participation by the same user
- screenshot / code sharing
- “freebie hunter” audiences claiming rewards but never converting
- coupon abuse in digital channels

For field agencies, this is also a control problem: once physical product is on-site, shrink and unauthorized giveaway become hard to monitor.

### Source-backed signals

- Sampl repeatedly cites the problem of **freebie hunters** and the need to filter low-quality requests:
  [Sampl platform](https://www.sampltech.com/)
  [Dr Barbara Sturm case](https://www.sampltech.com/case-studies/dr-barbara-sturm-highly-targeted-international-sampling)
  [Cadbury case](https://www.sampltech.com/case-studies/how-cadbury-harnessed-product-sampling)
- Andrex’s case shows large-scale filtering: over **35,000 requests** came in and **20,000 were filtered out**:
  [Andrex case](https://www.sampltech.com/case-studies/heres-how-we-helped-andrex-r-capture-genuine-reviews)
- PedidosYa said coupons were **costly due to fraud** in its old system:
  [PedidosYa + Talon.One](https://www.talon.one/customers/pedidosya-deliver-targeted-campaigns-with-talon-one)
- Voucherify also describes promotion fraud as a growing challenge in incentive-based campaigns:
  [Stop Promotion Fraud with Wyng and AtData](https://www.voucherify.io/promotion-examples/stop-promotion-fraud-wyng-atdata)

### How brands try to solve it

- unique codes
- short expiry windows
- one-use rules
- user validation
- rate limiting
- audience qualification before sample approval
- digital activation only at the moment of redemption

### Does it work?

Yes, but only if the controls are designed into the mechanic.

Successful patterns:

- SamplMatch-style pre-qualification reduces low-quality claims:
  [Dr Barbara Sturm case](https://www.sampltech.com/case-studies/dr-barbara-sturm-highly-targeted-international-sampling)
- rule-based promotion engines reduce abuse in coupon systems:
  [PedidosYa + Talon.One](https://www.talon.one/customers/pedidosya-deliver-targeted-campaigns-with-talon-one)

### Bottom line

For physical promo points, this is likely an even bigger problem than in public case studies, because product can be stolen or given out off-system. That last point is an inference, but it is consistent with why brands invest in controlled redemption and real-time logging.

## 4. Retail execution and stock availability

### Why this matters

A digital coupon or sample mechanic fails if:

- the product is not on shelf
- the cashier does not know the mechanic
- the store cannot redeem the code
- the consumer is sent to a location where the item is unavailable

This is a serious problem in Kazakhstan / CIS because distribution coverage and retail maturity are uneven by city, chain, and format.

### Source-backed signals

- NIQ highlights out-of-stocks as a direct source of missed promotional opportunities:
  [OSA Barometer](https://nielseniq.com/global/en/products/osa-barometer/)
- NIQ also emphasizes the need for real-time monitoring of retail execution:
  [In-Store Vision](https://nielseniq.com/global/en/products/nielseniq-instore-vision/)
- Sampl says one of the major problems is converting trial to purchase when the consumer is not clearly guided to a store:
  [NearStore blog](https://www.sampltech.com/blog/how-nearstore-links-digital-sampling-with-in-store-sales)
- Kazakhstan retail remains mixed: in 2025, the statistics bureau reported that retail sales by **individual entrepreneurs, including those trading in markets, increased 16.4%** in Q1 2025, which suggests a still-important traditional / fragmented trade component:
  [Kazakhstan domestic trade January-March 2025](https://stat.gov.kz/en/industries/economy/local-market/publications/349885/)

### How brands try to solve it

- limit campaigns to specific chains and SKUs
- geofence around stores where the product is available
- use store locator / NearStore logic
- reconcile with store-level stock and availability reports
- use retail execution audits

### Does it work?

Yes, when the campaign is tightly scoped.

- Manilife targeted only users living near stores where the product was available and reported **40% uplift in rate of sale**:
  [Manilife case](https://www.sampltech.com/case-studies/how-we-helped-manilife-with-their-healthy-eating-campaign)

### Bottom line

In Kazakhstan, a pilot should stay narrow geographically and by partner chain. Otherwise the campaign risks sending people into dead ends.

## 5. Staffing and field-force reliability

### Why this matters

Classic sampling needs:

- promoters
- supervisors
- people to transport and store product
- people to count and report results

Promo agencies absorb this complexity, but they also inherit staffing risk:

- seasonal staffing gaps
- inconsistent training
- compliance drift
- manual reporting

### Kazakhstan / CIS specifics

Kazakhstan has documented labor shortages, including for sellers, supervisors, drivers, movers, and similar roles that are adjacent to promo execution.

### Source-backed signals

- Atameken reported that **one in ten Kazakhstani companies** struggles with staff shortage, and named shortages in roles like sellers, store supervisors, drivers, movers, and security:
  [One in Ten Kazakhstani Companies Struggle with Staff Shortage](https://kz.kursiv.media/en/2020-03-04/one-ten-kazakhstani-companies-struggle-staff-shortage/)

### How brands try to solve it

- reduce dependence on staffed sampling
- move from physical handout to digital voucher claim
- centralize control in bot / app / web panel
- use promoter only where human interaction is essential

### Does it work?

Yes, structurally.

This is one of the clearest advantages of digital redemption models: they remove large parts of the manual field-force burden. The evidence here is more inferential than direct, but it aligns with why digital sampling platforms pitch themselves as more measurable and less operationally heavy.

### Bottom line

For Kazakhstan/CIS, reducing promoter dependence is a meaningful operational advantage, not a cosmetic feature.

## 6. Market fragmentation and channel coverage

### Why this matters

Even if a mechanic works in a modern chain, it may not scale smoothly across:

- traditional trade
- smaller cities
- fragmented distribution
- audiences without the chosen digital channel

### Kazakhstan / CIS specifics

- Kazakhstan retail still has a meaningful non-chain and market component.
- In some categories, chain retail penetration remains limited.
- International retailers have sometimes struggled to fit local assortment, pricing, and format expectations.

### Source-backed signals

- Kazakhstan domestic trade data shows significant activity outside large trading enterprises:
  [Kazakhstan domestic trade January-March 2025](https://stat.gov.kz/en/industries/economy/local-market/publications/349885/)
- DairyNews noted chain retail in one frozen category was still only **15–20%** of the sector:
  [Ice Cream Market in Kazakhstan – First Half of 2024](https://dairynews.today/news/ice-cream-market-in-kazakhstan-first-half-of-2024.html)
- Kursiv’s reporting on foreign retail entry stresses local assortment and purchasing-power fit as real issues:
  [VkusVill opens first store in Kazakhstan](https://kz.kursiv.media/en/2024-04-23/russian-vkusvill-opens-its-first-store-in-kazakhstan/amp/)

### How brands try to solve it

- pilot in major cities first
- use a small number of reliable partners
- adapt assortment and channel mix locally
- add fallback entry points later if a messenger-only flow excludes too much audience

### Does it work?

Usually yes, if the pilot is scoped properly.

### Bottom line

For Coca-Cola, this argues for a narrow Almaty / Astana / selected-chain pilot rather than a national launch.

## 7. Rising costs and supply volatility

### Why this matters

Sampling budgets get squeezed not only by agency fees, but by:

- transport cost inflation
- storage cost inflation
- staffing costs
- input volatility
- product supply interruptions

### Kazakhstan / CIS specifics

- Kazakhstan has seen logistics cost pressure and supply disruption tied to border issues and imported inputs.
- Beverage production has also been exposed to raw-material disruptions, such as sugar.

### Source-backed signals

- PMI and trade reporting point to higher costs for transportation, utilities, and customs-linked delays:
  [Manufacturing PMI / logistics costs](https://kz.kursiv.media/en/2025-03-03/engk-tank-manufacturing-industry-reports-increasing-business-activity-for-12-consecutive-months-in-kazakhstan/)
  [Services PMI / Russian border delays](https://kz.kursiv.media/en/2025-12-03/engk-tank-kazakhstans-service-sector-reports-activity-decline-amid-delays-at-the-russian-border/)
- Kazakhstan’s beverage industry previously faced disruption from sugar shortages:
  [Shortage of sugar threatens food industry in Kazakhstan](https://kz.kursiv.media/en/2022-05-05/shortage-of-sugar-threatens-food-industry-in-kazakhstan/)
  [Ministry says no sugar shortage expected this year](https://kz.kursiv.media/en/2025-03-13/engk-nknk-kazakhstans-ministry-of-trade-foresees-no-sugar-shortage-this-year/)

### How brands try to solve it

- avoid shipping full-size product to temporary points when possible
- use partner stock already in market
- keep pilots geographically narrow
- use digital mechanics that let the brand adjust quickly if costs or supply change

### Does it work?

Yes, but it does not remove macro risk. It mainly reduces campaign-level exposure.

### Bottom line

This problem matters less than logistics itself, but it directly worsens the economics of classic offline sampling.

## What brands are actually doing now

Across the research, the practical solution pattern is consistent:

### 1. Shift fulfillment to existing retail or delivery networks

This reduces:

- cold-chain burden
- temporary storage burden
- field transportation burden

Examples:

- Sampl / NearStore
- Bio&Me / Tesco
- retail-linked coupon and cashback models

### 2. Add a digital claim or activation layer before redemption

This improves:

- measurement
- anti-fraud
- CRM capture
- real-time visibility

Examples:

- Sampl platform and case studies
- SamplPay cashback
- promotion engines like Talon.One

### 3. Tighten eligibility and audience quality

This reduces:

- freebie hunters
- wasted samples
- poor lead quality

Examples:

- SamplMatch filtering in Andrex, Cadbury, Dr Barbara Sturm

### 4. Scope campaigns to stores where the product is actually available

This improves:

- conversion
- consumer experience
- retailer relations

Examples:

- NearStore
- Manilife / Sainsbury’s targeting

---

## Overall judgment

For Coca-Cola and a promo agency in Kazakhstan/CIS, the biggest pain points in sampling are:

- physical execution
- cost of controlling physical execution
- lack of real-time measurement
- fraud / abuse / shrinkage

The strongest current solution pattern is **not** "better staffed sampling."
It is **hybrid digital sampling**:

- digital claim
- controlled activation
- redemption through retail / delivery / tightly managed promo points

That approach does not eliminate every problem, but it addresses the top-ranked ones better than classic promoter-led sampling.
